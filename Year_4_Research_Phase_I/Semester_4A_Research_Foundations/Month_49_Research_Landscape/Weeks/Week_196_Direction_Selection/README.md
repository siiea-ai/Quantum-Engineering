## Resources

### Decision-Making Framework

**Step 1: Ensure Complete Information**
- All problem profiles complete
- All scores assigned
- Advisor input received

**Step 2: Apply Quantitative Comparison**
- Calculate weighted scores
- Rank problems by score
- Identify clear leaders

**Step 3: Apply Qualitative Filters**
- Does this excite you?
- Can you imagine working on this for years?
- Does your advisor support this?
- Is this feasible given constraints?

**Step 4: Make the Decision**
- Choose your primary direction
- Identify backup directions
- Commit (for now) and move forward

**Step 5: Document Your Reasoning**
- Why this direction over alternatives?
- What convinced you?
- What concerns remain?

---

### Common Decision Challenges

**Challenge: Multiple good options**
- This is a good problem to have
- Trust your excitement/interest
- Remember: any of them is probably fine
- You can always pivot later

**Challenge: No option feels right**
- Revisit your priorities and weights
- Consider hybrid approaches
- Talk to advisor for perspective
- Consider whether you need more exploration

**Challenge: Head vs. heart conflict**
- Take the heart seriously
- Motivation sustains effort
- But don't ignore practical concerns
- Look for a synthesis

**Challenge: Fear of commitment**
- Research directions evolve
- You're not locked in forever
- Decide and iterate
- Action beats analysis paralysis

## Tips for Writing Your Statement

### What Makes a Good Statement

**Clarity:** The reader should understand exactly what you want to work on

**Motivation:** The reader should understand why this matters

**Credibility:** The reader should believe you can do this

**Engagement:** The reader should want to keep reading

**Specificity:** Avoid vague generalities; be concrete

### Common Mistakes

1. **Too broad:** "I want to study quantum computing"
2. **Too narrow:** Technical details without context
3. **No motivation:** What problem are you solving?
4. **No personal connection:** Why you?
5. **Overselling:** Unrealistic claims about impact
6. **Underselling:** Not conveying why this is interesting
7. **Poor writing:** Unclear sentences, typos, jargon

### Writing Process Tips

1. **Start with your problem statement** - Get this crystal clear first
2. **Build context around it** - What does the reader need to know?
3. **Connect to impact** - So what?
4. **Add personal elements** - Why you?
5. **Revise, revise, revise** - First drafts are always rough
