# Guide: Decision-Making and Research Interest Statement Writing

## Introduction

Week 196 requires two distinct but connected skills: making a good decision about your research direction, and articulating that decision in a compelling written statement. This guide provides comprehensive methodology for both.

The decision and its articulation reinforce each other: writing forces clarity, and clarity improves decisions.

---

## Part 1: The Decision-Making Process

### 1.1 Why Decisions Are Hard

Research direction decisions are challenging because:
- **Multiple viable options** - Several paths could lead to success
- **Imperfect information** - You can't fully know what each path holds
- **Long-term commitment** - The choice shapes years of your life
- **Identity involvement** - It becomes part of who you are

These challenges are normal. The goal isn't to find the "perfect" choice but to make a good choice and commit to it.

### 1.2 The Good-Enough Decision

For PhD research directions, the standard is not perfection:

**A good choice:**
- Is tractable (you can make progress)
- Is interesting (to you and others)
- Has available resources
- Has advisor support
- Leads somewhere useful

**Any of your shortlisted problems probably qualifies.** The differences between them are usually smaller than they appear.

### 1.3 Decision Framework

**Stage 1: Preparation (Days 1366-1368)**

Gather all inputs:
- [ ] Problem profiles complete and current
- [ ] Advisor feedback integrated
- [ ] Resource constraints understood
- [ ] Skills gaps identified

**Stage 2: Comparison (Day 1366)**

Apply structured comparison:

| Criterion | Weight | P1 | P2 | P3 | P4 | P5 |
|-----------|--------|----|----|----|----|-----|
| Importance | 20% | | | | | |
| Tractability | 25% | | | | | |
| Resource match | 20% | | | | | |
| Excitement | 20% | | | | | |
| Advisor support | 15% | | | | | |
| **Weighted Total** | | | | | | |

**Stage 3: Qualitative Check (Day 1366)**

For top 2-3 candidates, answer:
1. Can I imagine working on this for 3+ years?
2. Would I be proud of solving this?
3. Does this lead to careers I want?
4. Does my gut say yes?

**Stage 4: Selection (Day 1366)**

Make your choice:
- **Primary direction:** Your main focus
- **Secondary direction:** Backup or complementary project
- **Tertiary direction:** Fallback if others fail

**Stage 5: Commitment (Day 1366+)**

- Write down your choice and reasoning
- Tell someone (advisor, peer)
- Start acting on it (drafting statement)
- Stop second-guessing (for now)

### 1.4 Dealing with Uncertainty

**Accept that uncertainty remains.** You cannot eliminate it. But you can:

1. **Identify remaining questions**
   - What would you need to know?
   - Can you find out during Month 50?

2. **Create checkpoints**
   - When will you reassess?
   - What would trigger reconsideration?

3. **Define experiments**
   - Can you test your direction quickly?
   - What would a 1-month pilot look like?

4. **Trust the process**
   - You've done thorough analysis
   - Your shortlist contains viable options
   - Making progress will reveal more information

## Part 2: Understanding Research Interest Statements

### 2.1 What is a Research Interest Statement?

A research interest statement is a concise document that:
- Describes what you want to research
- Explains why it matters
- Outlines how you might approach it
- Demonstrates why you're suited to do it

**It is NOT:**
- A detailed research proposal
- A literature review
- A comprehensive background
- A formal grant application

### 2.2 Purposes of the Statement

**For You:**
- Crystallizes your thinking
- Forces articulation of ideas
- Creates reference document
- Establishes baseline for evolution

**For Your Advisor:**
- Communicates your direction
- Shows readiness for research
- Enables targeted guidance
- Sets expectations

**For Your Program:**
- Demonstrates progress
- Supports qualifying requirements
- Documents research maturation

### 2.3 Audience Considerations

Your primary audience is typically:
- Your advisor (expert in your general area)
- Committee members (experts in adjacent areas)
- Program administrators (may be non-specialists)

Write for **intelligent non-specialists** in your exact subarea:
- Assume graduate-level technical background
- Explain specialized terminology
- Don't assume familiarity with your specific problem
- Provide sufficient context

## Part 3: Statement Structure and Content

### 3.1 Overall Structure

**Recommended Length:** 2-3 pages, single-spaced (~1000-1500 words)

**Standard Structure:**

1. **Opening Hook** (1-2 paragraphs, ~150 words)
2. **Background/Context** (2-3 paragraphs, ~300 words)
3. **Problem Statement** (1-2 paragraphs, ~200 words)
4. **Approach/Interests** (2-3 paragraphs, ~300 words)
5. **Significance/Impact** (1-2 paragraphs, ~150 words)
6. **Personal Fit** (1-2 paragraphs, ~150 words)
7. **Conclusion** (1 paragraph, ~100 words)

### 3.2 Section-by-Section Guidance

#### Opening Hook

**Purpose:** Capture attention and establish relevance

**Strategies:**
- Start with a compelling question
- Open with a striking fact or statistic
- Begin with a vision of the future
- Lead with a concrete problem

**Example Opening:**
> "Quantum computers promise to solve problems that would take classical computers longer than the age of the universe—but only if we can protect quantum information from errors. My research focuses on developing practical decoder algorithms for quantum error correction, a critical piece of the puzzle that stands between today's noisy quantum devices and tomorrow's fault-tolerant quantum computers."

**What to Avoid:**
- "Since ancient times, humans have wondered..."
- "In this statement, I will describe..."
- Dry, generic openings
- Excessive jargon

#### Background/Context

**Purpose:** Give the reader sufficient context to understand your problem

**Include:**
- Relevant field overview
- Current state of research
- What has been achieved
- What challenges remain

**Keep It:**
- Concise (not a literature review)
- Focused (only relevant background)
- Accurate (cite sources)
- Accessible (explain terms)

**Balance:** Enough context to understand the problem, but not so much that you lose the reader.

#### Problem Statement

**Purpose:** Clearly articulate what you want to investigate

**This is the heart of your statement.** Be:
- **Specific:** What exactly are you interested in?
- **Clear:** Could someone else understand and pursue this?
- **Bounded:** What's in scope vs. out of scope?

**Structure:**
1. What is the problem?
2. Why is it hard/unsolved?
3. Why is it important to solve?

**Example:**
> "I aim to develop neural network-based decoders for surface codes that can operate in real-time on current hardware. While theoretical decoders exist, they are either too slow for practical use (maximum likelihood) or insufficiently accurate for low error rates (simple matching). My goal is to bridge this gap by designing decoders that achieve near-optimal accuracy while meeting the sub-microsecond timing constraints of real quantum hardware."

#### Approach/Interests

**Purpose:** Describe how you might tackle the problem and what specific aspects interest you

**This section shows you've thought beyond just identifying a problem.**

**Include:**
- Potential methodological approaches
- Specific technical directions
- Related questions you find interesting
- How your work might unfold

**Note:** This is not a detailed methodology. You're indicating directions, not committing to specific plans.

#### Significance/Impact

**Purpose:** Explain why solving this problem matters

**Address:**
- Scientific significance
- Practical applications
- Broader impact on the field
- Who would care about your results

**Connect:** Link back to your opening hook—make it clear how your work addresses the challenge you opened with.

#### Personal Fit

**Purpose:** Explain why you're the right person for this research

**Include:**
- Relevant background and training
- Skills you bring
- Previous experience
- Genuine enthusiasm and motivation

**Show, don't tell:** Instead of "I am passionate," describe what draws you to this work.

#### Conclusion

**Purpose:** Close strong with forward-looking perspective

**Include:**
- Summary of direction
- Near-term next steps
- Long-term vision
- Closing hook

## Part 4: Writing Process

### 4.1 Pre-Writing

Before drafting:

1. **Gather materials:**
   - Problem profiles
   - Key paper citations
   - Notes and insights
   - Advisor feedback

2. **Create outline:**
   - List main points for each section
   - Identify key citations
   - Note transitions

3. **Write problem statement first:**
   - Get this crystal clear before anything else
   - Everything else builds around it

### 4.2 Drafting

**First Draft Strategy:**

1. **Write quickly** - Don't agonize over word choice
2. **Follow your outline** - But deviate if ideas flow
3. **Aim for completeness** - Get all sections down
4. **Don't edit yet** - Resist the urge to polish mid-draft

**Time:** Plan 3-4 hours for first draft

### 4.3 Revision

**Revision Rounds:**

**Round 1: Structure and Content**
- Is each section doing its job?
- Is the problem clear?
- Is anything missing?
- Is anything unnecessary?

**Round 2: Argument and Flow**
- Does each paragraph lead to the next?
- Is the logic clear?
- Are claims supported?

**Round 3: Clarity and Precision**
- Is every sentence clear?
- Can anything be said more simply?
- Is jargon explained?

**Round 4: Polish**
- Grammar and spelling
- Citation formatting
- Consistent style

### 4.4 Feedback

**Peer Review:**
- Exchange with 2-3 peers
- Ask for specific feedback types
- Give as carefully as you want to receive

**Advisor Review:**
- Share near-final draft
- Ask for content and direction feedback
- Incorporate suggestions thoughtfully

## Part 5: Common Pitfalls and Solutions

### 5.1 Content Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Example | Solution |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Too vague | "I want to improve quantum error correction" | Specify what aspect, how, why |
| Too technical | Dense equations without context | Explain in words first, then formalize |
| Too ambitious | "I will solve the scalability problem" | Be realistic about scope |
| No motivation | Jumping to problem without context | Build up to problem statement |
| No personal voice | Reads like a textbook | Add your perspective and interests |

### 5.2 Structure Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Example | Solution |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Weak opening | "In this statement I will..." | Start with hook |
| Buried problem | Problem appears on page 2 | Move up, make prominent |
| Missing sections | No personal fit section | Include all elements |
| Unbalanced | 2 pages of background, 1 paragraph of problem | Adjust proportions |

### 5.3 Writing Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Example | Solution |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Passive voice overuse | "It was shown that..." | Use active voice |
| Jargon without explanation | "qLDPC codes have good parameters" | Define terms |
| Run-on sentences | 50+ word sentences | Break up, simplify |
| Hedging | "I perhaps might try to..." | Be more direct |

## Part 6: Revision Checklist

Before finalizing, check:

### Content Checklist
- [ ] Problem is clearly stated
- [ ] Context is sufficient but not excessive
- [ ] Approach is outlined
- [ ] Significance is explained
- [ ] Personal fit is demonstrated
- [ ] All claims are supported

### Structure Checklist
- [ ] Opening is engaging
- [ ] Flow is logical
- [ ] Transitions are smooth
- [ ] Conclusion is strong
- [ ] Length is appropriate (2-3 pages)

### Writing Checklist
- [ ] Language is clear and accessible
- [ ] Sentences are well-constructed
- [ ] Paragraphs are focused
- [ ] No unnecessary jargon
- [ ] Active voice predominates

### Technical Checklist
- [ ] Citations are complete and formatted
- [ ] Terminology is consistent
- [ ] No typos or grammatical errors
- [ ] Formatting is professional

## Part 7: After the Statement

### 7.1 What Happens Next

Your statement is a living document that will evolve:

**Month 50:** Deep literature reading may refine your direction
**Month 51:** Problem formulation may narrow your focus
**Month 52:** Research proposal will add detail

**Keep your statement updated** as your thinking evolves.

### 7.2 Using Your Statement

**For advisor meetings:** Reference it to track evolution
**For collaborations:** Explains your interests quickly
**For applications:** Foundation for grant applications
**For yourself:** Reminder of your direction and motivation

## Conclusion

The Week 196 decision and statement represent a milestone in your transition to researcher. The key principles are:

1. **Make a decision** - Commit to a direction, even with uncertainty
2. **Articulate clearly** - Force yourself to write it down
3. **Seek feedback** - Others will see what you miss
4. **Revise thoroughly** - Good writing is rewriting
5. **Stay flexible** - Your statement will evolve

By week's end, you should have a clear research direction and a polished statement that communicates it effectively. This provides the foundation for all your subsequent research work.

## Appendix: Example Statement Outlines

### Example 1: Theory-Focused Statement

**Problem:** Novel decoder algorithms for qLDPC codes

1. Opening: The promise of fault-tolerant QC
2. Background: QEC and decoder landscape
3. Problem: qLDPC codes lack efficient decoders
4. Approach: Combine belief propagation with neural networks
5. Significance: Enables practical good codes
6. Fit: ML + QEC background
7. Conclusion: Path to fault-tolerant QC

### Example 2: Experimental-Theory Interface

**Problem:** Characterizing noise in neutral atom systems

1. Opening: Neutral atoms as scalable quantum platform
2. Background: Neutral atom hardware and noise sources
3. Problem: Noise characterization for QEC
4. Approach: Develop tailored benchmarking protocols
5. Significance: Enables better error correction
6. Fit: Physics + computation background
7. Conclusion: Bridging hardware and QEC

### Example 3: Applications-Focused Statement

**Problem:** Quantum algorithms for materials simulation

1. Opening: Materials discovery challenge
2. Background: Classical simulation limits, quantum opportunity
3. Problem: Resource requirements too high for near-term
4. Approach: Develop more efficient encodings and algorithms
5. Significance: Enables practical quantum advantage
6. Fit: Chemistry + quantum computing training
7. Conclusion: Path to useful quantum chemistry
