## What is a Research Gap?

A research gap is a space where:
- Knowledge is incomplete or missing
- Methods don't exist or are inadequate
- Theory and practice diverge
- Different results conflict
- Natural next steps haven't been taken

**Not all gaps are good research opportunities.** A good research problem is a gap that is:
- **Important** - Solving it matters to the community
- **Tractable** - Progress is achievable with available resources
- **Timely** - The field is ready for this problem to be solved
- **Appropriate** - Matches your skills, interests, and timeline

### Day 1363 (Friday): Problem Characterization

**Objective:** Assess tractability and impact of identified problems

**Morning (3 hours): Tractability Analysis**
For each promising gap, evaluate:
- Is the problem well-defined?
- What tools/methods might apply?
- What has been tried before?
- Why might you succeed where others haven't?
- Can meaningful progress be made in 2-4 years?

**Afternoon (2 hours): Impact Analysis**
For each promising gap, evaluate:
- What would solving this enable?
- Who would care about the solution?
- Would it be publishable (where)?
- Does it have practical implications?
- Does it open new research directions?

**Evening (2 hours): Resource Matching**
For each promising gap, evaluate:
- Do I have the necessary skills?
- Are computational resources available?
- Is relevant expertise accessible?
- Are collaborators/advisors available?
- What would I need to acquire?

## Resources

### Gap Identification Checklist

When reading papers, look for:
- [ ] "Future work" sections
- [ ] "Limitations" sections
- [ ] "Open questions" or "challenges"
- [ ] Phrases like "it remains unknown," "further study needed"
- [ ] Discrepancies between papers
- [ ] Missing connections to other work
- [ ] Scalability discussions
- [ ] Assumptions that could be relaxed

### Problem Quality Indicators

**Good Problems:**
- Clearly defined scope
- Progress would be recognizable
- Builds on existing tools/methods
- Multiple potential approaches
- Fits PhD timeline (2-4 years for main contribution)
- Advisor has relevant expertise
- Likely to lead to publications

**Risky Problems:**
- Vaguely defined
- Success criteria unclear
- Requires non-existent tools
- Only one approach possible
- Too large for PhD scope
- No local expertise
- Unknown if publishable

## Tips for Effective Gap Analysis

### Common Patterns

**Where Gaps Hide:**
1. At boundaries between subareas
2. In "obvious" next steps nobody has taken
3. In applications of methods to new domains
4. In scaling small results to larger systems
5. In connecting theory to experiment

**Warning Signs of Pseudo-Gaps:**
1. Solved in literature you haven't found
2. Too hard (everyone has tried and failed)
3. Not actually interesting to the community
4. Requires resources nobody has
5. Solves itself with normal field progress

### Validation Strategies

**Before committing to a gap:**
1. Search extensively (Google Scholar, arXiv) to confirm it's open
2. Check if leading groups are working on it
3. Ask experienced researchers if it's real
4. Consider why it hasn't been solved
5. Estimate what solving it would require
