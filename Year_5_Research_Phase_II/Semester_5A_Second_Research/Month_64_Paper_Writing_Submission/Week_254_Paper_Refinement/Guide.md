# Week 254: Paper Refinement

## Days 1772-1778 | Elevating Draft Quality Through Systematic Revision

---

## Overview

Week 254 transforms your complete first draft into a polished manuscript. Revision is not editing—it is re-visioning your work, strengthening arguments, clarifying exposition, and ensuring every element serves the paper's core message. Professional papers typically undergo 5-10 revision passes before submission.

### Week Objectives

By the end of Week 254, you will have:

1. Completed multiple revision passes at different levels (structure, content, style)
2. Strengthened the logical flow and argument structure
3. Polished all figures to publication quality
4. Tightened prose and eliminated redundancy
5. Prepared a manuscript ready for final formatting

---

## The Revision Philosophy

### Why Revision Matters

First drafts capture ideas; revisions make them communicable. Even experienced authors produce rough first drafts. What separates published work from abandoned manuscripts is the revision process.

**The Revision Hierarchy**:

```
Level 1: GLOBAL STRUCTURE
    ↓
Level 2: ARGUMENT LOGIC
    ↓
Level 3: PARAGRAPH UNITY
    ↓
Level 4: SENTENCE CLARITY
    ↓
Level 5: WORD CHOICE
    ↓
Level 6: TECHNICAL POLISH
```

Work from global to local—there's no point perfecting sentences in a section that should be deleted.

## Day-by-Day Structure

### Day 1772 (Monday): Structural Review

**Morning (3 hours): The 30,000-Foot View**

Before revising sentences, verify the overall structure works.

**Structural Review Protocol**:

1. **Read the Entire Draft in One Sitting** (without editing)
   - Print it out if possible
   - Read as if you were a reviewer
   - Note gut reactions in margins
   - Don't stop to fix—just mark

2. **Create a Reverse Outline**

   After reading, write one sentence summarizing each paragraph:

   ```
   Introduction:
   ¶1: [Quantum computing promises exponential speedup]
   ¶2: [Error correction is essential for scalability]
   ¶3: [Surface codes are promising but threshold is challenging]
   ¶4: [We demonstrate below-threshold operation]

   Methods:
   ¶1: [System is 17 transmon qubits in surface code layout]
   ...
   ```

3. **Analyze the Reverse Outline**

   Ask these questions:
   - Does each paragraph have one clear point?
   - Do paragraphs appear in logical order?
   - Are there gaps in the argument?
   - Is anything redundant?
   - Is the structure appropriate for the paper type?

**Afternoon (3 hours): Structural Revision**

Based on your analysis, make structural changes:

**Common Structural Issues and Solutions**:

| Issue | Symptom | Solution |
|-------|---------|----------|
| Buried lead | Main finding not in first paragraph of Results | Reorder to lead with primary result |
| Wandering introduction | Intro reviews everything, loses focus | Cut tangential background |
| Disconnected sections | Sections don't flow into each other | Add transition paragraphs |
| Results-Discussion confusion | Interpretation mixed into Results | Move interpretation to Discussion |
| Conclusion as summary | Conclusion just repeats earlier content | Synthesize instead of summarize |
| Anticlimactic order | Strongest result appears late | Reorder for impact |

**Structural Revision Checklist**:
- [ ] Introduction establishes clear motivation
- [ ] Methods are complete and reproducible
- [ ] Results are logically ordered
- [ ] Discussion interprets without repeating
- [ ] Conclusion provides synthesis and outlook
- [ ] Each section transitions smoothly to the next

**Evening (1 hour): Document Restructuring**

Implement major structural changes identified. Don't worry about smooth prose yet—focus on getting sections in the right order with the right content.

### Day 1773 (Tuesday): Argument Strengthening

**Morning (3 hours): Logical Flow Analysis**

Every paper makes an argument. Strengthen yours.

**Argument Mapping Exercise**:

1. Write your main claim in one sentence
2. List the evidence supporting this claim
3. Identify logical connections between evidence and claim
4. Find gaps or weaknesses in the chain

```
Main Claim: [Our error correction protocol achieves below-threshold operation]
                              ↓
    Evidence 1: [Logical error rate decreases with code distance]
                              ↓
    Evidence 2: [Threshold estimated at p_th = 0.95%]
                              ↓
    Evidence 3: [Physical error rate measured at 0.5% < p_th]
                              ↓
    Supporting: [Theory predicts threshold, our data matches]
                              ↓
    Addressing counter: [Alternative explanations ruled out]
```

**Strengthening Techniques**:

1. **Lead with Your Strongest Evidence**
   Place your most compelling result prominently

2. **Acknowledge and Address Weaknesses**
   Rather than hiding limitations, discuss them honestly

3. **Quantify Everything Possible**
   "Significantly improved" → "Improved by a factor of 3.5"

4. **Connect to Theory**
   Explain why your results make sense theoretically

5. **Compare Fairly**
   Benchmark against prior art using consistent metrics

**Afternoon (3 hours): Evidence Review**

For each major claim, verify:
- Is the evidence sufficient?
- Are error bars included?
- Is statistical analysis appropriate?
- Are controls adequate?
- Are alternative explanations addressed?

**Red Flags to Address**:
- Claims without quantitative support
- Results described as "good" without comparison
- Error bars missing from key measurements
- Statistical tests not specified
- Assumptions stated but not justified

**Evening (1 hour): Claim-Evidence Alignment**

Revise text to strengthen claim-evidence connections. Use language that makes logical relationships explicit:

- "This demonstrates that..."
- "Consistent with theoretical predictions..."
- "These results establish..."
- "The correlation between X and Y suggests..."

### Day 1774 (Wednesday): Introduction and Discussion Polish

**Morning (3 hours): Introduction Refinement**

The introduction is your reader's first impression. Make it count.

**Introduction Revision Checklist**:

| Element | Present? | Effective? | Revisions Needed |
|---------|----------|------------|------------------|
| Opening hook | [ ] | [ ] | |
| Problem importance | [ ] | [ ] | |
| Prior work review | [ ] | [ ] | |
| Gap identification | [ ] | [ ] | |
| Contribution statement | [ ] | [ ] | |
| Paper roadmap | [ ] | [ ] | |

**Opening Paragraph Revision**:

The first sentence should:
- Capture attention
- Establish context
- Be accessible to broad physics audience

Compare:
> ❌ "Quantum computing has been extensively studied due to its potential for solving complex problems."

> ✅ "Protecting quantum information from decoherence remains the central challenge in building a useful quantum computer."

The first is vague; the second is specific and establishes the paper's focus.

**Prior Work Section Balance**:

Review your literature discussion for:
- **Completeness**: Are key papers cited?
- **Fairness**: Do you acknowledge others' contributions?
- **Focus**: Do citations support your narrative?
- **Recency**: Are recent developments included?
- **Balance**: Not too sparse or too extensive?

**Afternoon (3 hours): Discussion Enhancement**

The Discussion is where you demonstrate scientific maturity.

**Discussion Components Checklist**:

1. **Interpretation of Results**
   - [ ] What do findings mean?
   - [ ] Connection to theoretical expectations?
   - [ ] Explanation of surprises?

2. **Context and Comparison**
   - [ ] How do results compare to prior work?
   - [ ] Quantitative comparison if possible?
   - [ ] Advantages/disadvantages honestly stated?

3. **Limitations**
   - [ ] Experimental/computational constraints acknowledged?
   - [ ] Scope of applicability clear?
   - [ ] Assumptions stated explicitly?

4. **Implications**
   - [ ] What does this mean for the field?
   - [ ] Practical applications?
   - [ ] Theoretical insights?

5. **Future Directions**
   - [ ] Natural extensions identified?
   - [ ] Open questions raised?
   - [ ] Concrete next steps?

**Handling Limitations Professionally**:

Acknowledging limitations builds credibility. Frame them constructively:

> "While our measurements were performed at base temperature (20 mK), extending this protocol to higher temperatures relevant to practical operation will require addressing thermal occupation of higher transmon levels."

**Evening (1 hour): Discussion-Introduction Consistency**

Verify that:
- Claims in Discussion align with Introduction promises
- Contribution statement is fulfilled
- Gap identified is filled by your work

### Day 1775 (Thursday): Methods and Results Clarity

**Morning (3 hours): Methods Section Review**

The Methods section enables reproducibility—the foundation of science.

**Reproducibility Audit**:

| Element | Included? | Sufficient Detail? |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| System parameters | [ ] | [ ] |
| Experimental setup | [ ] | [ ] |
| Numerical methods | [ ] | [ ] |
| Data analysis | [ ] | [ ] |
| Error estimation | [ ] | [ ] |
| Code availability | [ ] | [ ] |

**Methods Revision Strategies**:

1. **The Expert Test**: Could a postdoc in your field reproduce this?
2. **The Parameter Audit**: Is every number in your simulations stated?
3. **The Flow Test**: Do steps appear in logical/chronological order?

**What to Move to Supplement**:
- Lengthy derivations (reference key results, prove in supplement)
- Detailed calibration procedures
- Extended parameter tables
- Code listings

**Afternoon (3 hours): Results Section Refinement**

Results should present findings objectively and compellingly.

**Results Revision Strategies**:

1. **Lead with Findings**

   Every results paragraph should start with a finding:

   > ✅ "The logical error rate decreases exponentially with code distance (Fig. 2a)."

   > ❌ "Figure 2a shows the logical error rate as a function of code distance."

2. **Quantify Precisely**

   > ✅ "We achieve a two-qubit gate fidelity of 99.4 ± 0.1%"

   > ❌ "We achieve high two-qubit gate fidelity"

3. **Connect Results**

   Use transitions to show relationships:
   - "Building on this result..."
   - "To verify this finding, we also measured..."
   - "This observation prompted investigation of..."

4. **Reference Figures Effectively**

   Figures should appear near their discussion. Reference format:
   - "as shown in Fig. 2(a)" — mid-sentence
   - "(Fig. 2a)" — parenthetical
   - "Figure 2 shows..." — sentence start (for major figures)

**Evening (1 hour): Results Flow Check**

Read the Results section aloud. Mark any place where you:
- Lose the thread
- Need to re-read for understanding
- Wonder "so what?"

Address these in tomorrow's revision.

### Day 1776 (Friday): Figure Enhancement

**Morning (3 hours): Figure Quality Assessment**

Figures are often the first thing readers examine. Make them exceptional.

**Figure-by-Figure Audit**:

For each figure, assess:

| Criterion | Fig 1 | Fig 2 | Fig 3 | Fig 4 |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| Message clear | | | | |
| Readable at size | | | | |
| Colors accessible | | | | |
| Labels complete | | | | |
| Error bars shown | | | | |
| Legend useful | | | | |
| Caption complete | | | | |

**Common Figure Issues**:

| Issue | Solution |
|-------|----------|
| Text too small | Increase font to 8-10pt at print size |
| Colors confusing | Use colorblind-accessible palette |
| Too much data | Split into multiple panels or use supplement |
| Missing context | Add annotations, reference lines |
| Inconsistent style | Standardize across all figures |
| Low resolution | Regenerate at 300 DPI or use vector format |

**Afternoon (3 hours): Figure Polishing**

Implement improvements identified in the morning audit.

**Schematic Figure (typically Figure 1) Enhancement**:
- Clean geometric shapes
- Consistent line weights
- Clear labeling
- Logical visual flow
- Color coding explained

**Data Figure Enhancement**:
- Appropriate axis ranges
- Error bars on all points
- Legend visible but unobtrusive
- Key features annotated
- Fits shown with parameters

**Comparison Figure Enhancement**:
- Same axes for fair comparison
- Baseline/threshold lines
- Color/symbol distinction
- Clear legend identifying each dataset

**Evening (1 hour): Caption Refinement**

Polish all captions to be:
- Self-contained (understandable without main text)
- Complete (all symbols defined)
- Accurate (matches figure content)
- Concise (no unnecessary words)

**Caption Structure**:
1. Opening statement (what the figure shows)
2. Panel descriptions (a, b, c)
3. Key finding or observation
4. Technical details (parameters, error bars)

### Day 1777 (Saturday): Prose Polish and Style

**Morning (3 hours): Sentence-Level Revision**

Now that structure and content are solid, polish the prose.

**Sentence Clarity Techniques**:

1. **Subject-Verb Proximity**

   Keep subjects near their verbs:
   > ❌ "The fidelity, after correction for state preparation and measurement errors using the procedure described in the methods section, was measured."

   > ✅ "After correcting for SPAM errors, we measured the fidelity."

2. **Active Voice Default**

   Use passive only when necessary:
   > ✅ "We measured the coherence time." (active)
   > ✅ "The sample was cooled to 20 mK." (passive acceptable—focuses on sample)

3. **Concrete Language**

   Replace vague words:
   > ❌ "The error rates were significantly reduced."
   > ✅ "The error rates decreased by a factor of 4.2."

4. **One Idea Per Sentence**

   Split compound sentences when complex:
   > ❌ "We observe that the fidelity improves with increasing pulse amplitude, which is consistent with theoretical predictions based on the adiabatic theorem, although at the highest amplitudes we see saturation effects that may be due to leakage to non-computational states."

   > ✅ "We observe that fidelity improves with increasing pulse amplitude, consistent with adiabatic predictions. At the highest amplitudes, saturation appears, likely due to leakage to non-computational states."

**Afternoon (2 hours): Paragraph Revision**

Each paragraph should have:
- Topic sentence (main point)
- Supporting evidence/explanation
- Transition to next paragraph

**Paragraph Checklist**:
- [ ] One main idea per paragraph
- [ ] Topic sentence at beginning
- [ ] Logical progression of ideas
- [ ] Appropriate length (5-10 sentences typically)
- [ ] Clear connection to adjacent paragraphs

**Evening (2 hours): Word-Level Polish**

1. **Eliminate Redundancy**

   | Redundant | Concise |
   |-----------|---------|
   | "in order to" | "to" |
   | "the fact that" | "that" |
   | "it is interesting to note that" | [delete] |
   | "at the present time" | "now" |
   | "in close proximity to" | "near" |

2. **Strengthen Weak Words**

   | Weak | Strong |
   |------|--------|
   | "very" | [delete or use specific word] |
   | "really" | [delete] |
   | "basically" | [delete] |
   | "utilize" | "use" |
   | "methodology" | "method" |

3. **Check Technical Terminology**
   - Defined before use?
   - Used consistently?
   - Necessary (or can plain language work)?

### Day 1778 (Sunday): Final Draft Integration

**Morning (3 hours): Abstract and Title Refinement**

With the full paper revised, revisit abstract and title.

**Abstract Revision Checklist**:
- [ ] Reflects current paper content accurately
- [ ] Includes specific quantitative results
- [ ] Accessible opening sentence
- [ ] Clear statement of contribution
- [ ] No undefined acronyms
- [ ] Within word limit (150-250 words typically)

**Title Evaluation**:
- [ ] Captures main finding
- [ ] Searchable (includes key terms)
- [ ] Concise (10-15 words)
- [ ] Accurate (doesn't overclaim)
- [ ] Distinctive (not generic)

**Title Revision Exercise**:

Write 5 alternative titles, then choose the best:
1. [Descriptive]: "Error-Corrected Logical Operations in a Surface Code Processor"
2. [Result-focused]: "Below-Threshold Logical Error Rates in Superconducting Qubits"

4. [Technical]: "Surface Code Threshold Measurement in a 17-Qubit Array"
5. [Scope-indicating]: "Toward Scalable Quantum Error Correction"

**Afternoon (3 hours): Full Draft Read-Through**

Read the entire paper from beginning to end—one final time before declaring the draft complete.

**Read-Through Focus Areas**:

## Revision Resources

### Editing Tools

**Self-Editing Techniques**:
- Read aloud (catches awkward phrasing)
- Print and markup (different perspective than screen)
- Read backwards (catches typos)
- Change font/format (fresh eyes effect)

**Software Tools**:
- Grammarly (general grammar)
- Writefull (academic-specific suggestions)
- LanguageTool (open-source alternative)
- Hemingway Editor (readability)

### Feedback

**Before Seeking Feedback**:
1. Revise as much as you can yourself
2. Prepare specific questions for readers
3. Choose appropriate readers (expertise level)
4. Give readers sufficient time

**Processing Feedback**:
- Listen to understand, not to defend
- Take notes on reactions
- Look for patterns across readers
- Distinguish preference from necessity
- Make changes that improve the paper

## Common Revision Patterns

### The Second Opinion Test

If you're unsure about a passage:
1. Ask: "Does every reader need this?"
2. Ask: "Is there a simpler way to say this?"
3. If unsure, mark for feedback from collaborator

### The "So What?" Test

For each paragraph in Results and Discussion, ask "So what?" If you can't articulate why it matters, either:
- Add explanation of significance
- Move to supplementary material
- Delete

### The Competition Test

Imagine a competitor reviewing your paper. What would they:
- Question?
- Challenge?
- Misunderstand?

Address these proactively in your revision.
